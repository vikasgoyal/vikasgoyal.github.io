Award-Winning Failures: When Recognition Hides Weak Value

March 13, 2026 • 3 min read • Career

Some projects become internal success stories long before they become useful. They attract sponsors, collect awards, and create executive visibility, even when the underlying problem is weak, the design is overbuilt, or adoption never materializes.

That is the pattern behind what I call award-winning failures: initiatives that succeed politically while failing operationally. The organization rewards motion, novelty, and narrative, while real business value remains thin or absent.

Why this matters

This is not just a delivery problem. It is a governance problem. When organizations reward visibility more than evidence, they get better at launching projects than judging whether those projects should exist at all.

The same warning signs show up again and again:

  • The project solves the wrong problem.
  • The technology is more compelling than the business case.
  • The solution is overdesigned for the actual need.
  • User adoption stays weak, but reporting stays optimistic.

The better question

Strong leadership is not about defending every funded initiative. It is about asking earlier whether the work is important, measurable, and worth doing now. Often the highest-value decision is not building something impressive. It is stopping something unnecessary.

Recognition is easy to manufacture inside an organization. Sustained usefulness is not.
