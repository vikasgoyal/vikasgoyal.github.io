The city determines latitude, longitude, and timezone. Sunrise and sunset are calculated in the browser using a standard solar-position approximation, the Vedic clock starts counting from the most recent local sunrise, and the lifestyle sections interpret that day through a simplified hybrid of dinacharya and ritucharya guidance.

Reading The Vedic Clock

Units of time

In this model, the day is expressed through ghati, pala, and vipala. The display starts from sunrise rather than midnight, which is why the Vedic clock and the Gregorian clock do not share the same zero point.

Ghati
1 ghati = 24 modern minutes. 60 ghatis make one full day-night cycle.
Pala
1 pala = 24 seconds. 60 palas make 1 ghati.
Vipala
1 vipala = 0.4 seconds. 60 vipalas make 1 pala.

Traditional daily rhythm

In traditional Hindu practice, time is often treated as qualitative as well as numerical. A sunrise-based clock supports a day structure in which activities are aligned to natural light, ritual windows, and disciplined repetition.

Before and near sunrise: commonly associated with prayer, meditation, chanting, study, and preparation for the day.

Morning daylight: often regarded as suitable for focused learning, teaching, planning, and disciplined work.

Midday and afternoon: more commonly linked with worldly duties, administration, trade, travel, craft, and household responsibilities.

Sunset period: traditionally treated as another transition point for pause, prayer, reflection, and resetting the rhythm of the household.

Night: often reserved for lighter obligations, family, contemplation, and rest, rather than extending intense activity deep into the night.

These descriptions are broad cultural patterns, not strict universal rules. Different regions, traditions, and lineages interpret daily timing differently, especially when concepts such as muhurtas, sandhya periods, dosha-based routines, or temple schedules are used in more detail.
